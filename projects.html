Schloetter GmbH. - Predict sales volumes intelligently

Company and Data
Schlötter GmbH, headquartered in Geislingen, is one of the leading companies in electroplating technology in Germany. he company's core competencies include the development and manufacture of electroplating chemicals for surface coating and the construction of electroplating systems. Schlötter GmbH provided sales data spanning the last 13 years, comprising approximately one million data points. The data included detailed sales information for each product on a daily basis, such as sales volume, warehouse location, customer number, order time, delivery time, and more.
Problem
The sales volume of Schlötter products is influenced by numerous factors, including market competition, economic fluctuations, and marketing strategies. Consequently, producing accurate sales forecasts is a complex challenge. The presence of "surprise orders," where order volumes can suddenly spike to many times the usual amount, further complicates the forecasting process. Inaccurate forecasts can lead to significant losses: under-forecasting results in lost orders and emergency shipments, while over-forecasting leads to unnecessary storage costs.
Solution
To mitigate the losses associated with inaccurate forecasts, we optimized the prediction of sales volumes using advanced machine learning techniques. We began with a comprehensive data exploration, addressing key aspects such as outlier detection, seasonality analysis, correlation analysis, and the imputation of missing values. Once the data was cleaned and structured, we developed three categories of models:
Classic Time Series Forecasting Models: We employed traditional time series forecasting methods, such as ARIMA.
Traditional Machine Learning Models: We utilized algorithms such as Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Decision Trees. Thousands of features were extracted from the time series data using the tsfresh Python package. After feature selection, these features were used to train the models.
Deep Learning Models: We initially implemented RNN-based models. To better capture the influence and interaction between different products and regions, we enhanced the RNN models with self-attention modules and graph convolution layers. Additionally, to leverage long-term periodic information in handling long sequence inputs, we innovatively incorporated autocorrelation into the deep learning network.
Considering the significant impact of inaccurate forecasts, we employed a weighted loss function, where underprediction carried a higher penalty compared to overprediction. An asymmetric evaluation metric was defined to reflect this approach. Finally, the trained models were rigorously compared with Schlötter’s previous forecasting strategy.
Outcome
Through detailed analysis and extensive research, the pipeline we developed outperformed Schlötter's previous forecasting strategy, delivering more accurate predictions and significantly reducing the losses associated with forecasting errors.

Object recognition in technical drawings

Data and Problem
EDI GmbH has provided thousands of PDF versions of industrial drawings. The original CAD files for these drawings have been lost. Within these industrial drawings, different standards are used for surface specifications, dimensions, and references. The fonts of the product descriptions are not consistent, and due to changes in product descriptions, handwritten notes are often added. Manually retrieving this information is a very cumbersome process. EDI urgently needs an automated algorithm to extract information from these PDFs.
Problem
I approached this task as an image processing problem and set up a pipeline to extract key information from the PDFs. After converting the PDFs to images, I first performed image denoising and edge enhancement. Then, I located certain special symbols in the engineering drawings, such as diameter symbols. I employed two approaches here: one was a classic computer vision algorithm that used feature matching to locate the target positions, such as with SIFT; the other was an object detection algorithm using neural networks, primarily utilizing the YOLO series and the FRCNN series. To train these neural network models, I manually annotated the data. After locating the special symbols, I used line detection and OCR technologies to recognize the annotations corresponding to these symbols and saved the results in an Excel file.
Results
The trained model achieved excellent performance: the accuracy rate for recognizing the positions of dimensional entries with tolerances reached 93.3 percent. Beyond this success, I also proposed additional ideas to EDI, such as Sequence-to-Sequence Learning or combining the system with Graph Convolutional Models. Finally, they accepted ways to further improve the system, which take advantage of the dependency structure of labeling elements using Graph Convolutional Networks.
Company
ArtiMinds Robotics GmbH specializes in the development and distribution of software products aimed at standardizing and optimizing workflows for robotic automation. The software developed by ArtiMinds contains many embedded modules, which function as a type of digital assistant, allowing users to iteratively and flexibly adapt to individual manufacturing conditions.
Problem & Task
This project focused on optimizing the Through-Hole Technology (THT) assembly process. In through-hole assembly, electronic components with wire leads (such as integrated circuits) are inserted into contact holes on a printed circuit board (PCB) and then soldered. Inserting these components robotically without damaging the wire leads is a challenging task. The process requires precise location of the contact hole followed by careful insertion of the component. Typically, spiral search strategies are employed to locate the exact position of the hole. In this approach, the robot arm places the component at a predefined starting position on the PCB and then carefully moves it in a spiral path over the board until the correct target position is found. However, due to factors such as vibration-induced displacement, the relative positions of the starting points and target points can vary stochastically. These variations result in different search durations, and longer search times reduce the number of orders that can be completed within the specified timeframe. The objective of this project was to analyze the process data collected during assembly to optimize the process and minimize search durations.
Solution
After thoroughly analyzing the data, we developed an optimization method based on the distribution of search costs to determine the optimal starting position. The cost of the search is proportional to the length of the search path, with longer paths being more expensive. The goal of the algorithm is to identify the optimal starting position that minimizes the expected search cost across all previous searches, based on the historical data of start and end points. Recognizing that machine conditions change continuously, we assigned different weights to data points, with more recent data carrying greater weight. This approach involves several hyperparameters, so we designed experiments and evaluation criteria to optimize and select these parameters.
Results
By optimizing the starting position and search path, the average search time of the robot arm was significantly reduced. ArtiMinds can implement this method to dynamically determine and adjust the optimal starting position and search path during the production process, enhancing efficiency and flexibility.

Road Damage Detection

Task and Problem
Robert Bosch has developed cost-effective, high-frequency, and accurate vibration sensors to detect vehicle vibrations and monitor acceleration and deceleration events. Whenever a vehicle experiences vibrations exceeding a certain threshold, or during instances of sudden braking or sharp turning, the sensor data is recorded. These data not only provide insights into the vehicle's operational status, identifying potential anomalies or hazardous situations, but they also reflect the condition of the road surface. For example, when a pothole or other road damage is encountered, the vehicle may exhibit unusual vibrations, sudden braking, or swerving. The goal of this project is to explore the potential of using such sensor data for road damage detection. If these data can accurately identify road anomalies, the information can be used to notify vehicles through navigation systems or be reported to government authorities for repairs. Compared to using camera images for this task, sensor data offers several advantages, including easier data collection, smaller data size, faster transmission, and better privacy protection.
Solution
The task of detecting road damage based on vibration sensor data was framed as a multi-variate time series classification problem. Given the lack of labeled data and the substantial effort required for manual labeling, we employed a semi-supervised learning approach, which involved the following steps:
  1. Clustering and Labeling Centroids: We began with feature engineering on the time series data, extracting thousands of features across frequency domains, time domains, and statistical measures. Using hierarchical clustering, we grouped the time series data, and then labeled the centroid of each cluster along with a randomly selected subset of M samples.
  2. Building the Semi-supervised Model: We developed a Semi-supervised Deep Temporal Embedded Clustering (SDTEC) model, comprising two components: a temporal autoencoder and a temporal clustering network.
  3. Loss Function Design: The loss function for the model training was designed with three components: a reconstruction loss for all time series data, a clustering loss for all segments, and a classification loss for the labeled data.
  4. Model Training and Label Assignment: After training the model, the labels were assigned to the corresponding clusters based on the majority label of the samples within each cluster.
Results
The developed workflow significantly reduced the workload required for data labeling. The selected data samples were effectively providing the model with robust guidance for clustering unknown data. Ultimately, we observed that the segments clustered on the map were highly homogeneous, even though the model was not explicitly provided with location information. This highlights the effectiveness of the proposed approach in detecting and classifying road damage based on vibration sensor data.

Road Damage Detection

Task and Data
WEFA Singen GmbH, a member of the WEFA Group, specializes in the production of aluminum extrusion dies. To further enhance the quality of their products, WEFA sought to identify the key factors influencing product quality. The data provided by WEFA Singen came predominantly from their extrusion press production line and included a wide range of information such as descriptions of the target products, the raw materials used, and detailed records of the mould production process. The dataset was substantial, encompassing a physical volume of 1.3 GB with over 200 individual features and 20,000 different entries. Additionally, WEFA supplied a detailed prioritization of these features, offering valuable insights into their importance.
Solution
The primary objective was to determine the factors that significantly influence product quality and to understand the patterns or magnitudes of these influences. By doing so, we aimed to extract actionable rules that would allow WEFA Singen to integrate these findings with their extensive production expertise and apply them practically. To achieve this, we developed and trained two types of models. The first was a traditional neural network based on decision trees, which inherently offered a certain degree of interpretability. The second model was a deep learning neural network, a more complex "black box" model that, by nature, lacks interpretability. After training these models, we employed explainability algorithms such as LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) and SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to elucidate the predictions made by the models and to derive meaningful guidelines from the results. Understanding that not all stakeholders at WEFA had a background in machine learning, we also developed a visualization system. This system was designed to make the models and the derived guidelines more accessible and easier to comprehend, ensuring that even those without technical expertise could grasp the insights provided by the models.
Results
The experimental results were highly promising, demonstrating that the trained models predicted product quality with greater precision than WEFA's existing assessment methods. WEFA Singen GmbH was extremely satisfied with the outcomes, which led to the initiation of a subsequent two-year collaboration with our team.
